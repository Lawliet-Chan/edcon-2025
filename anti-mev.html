Anti-MEV

A Deep Dive into the Solutions and the Problems

1. Key Principles of Anti-MEV

Weakening the Power Asymmetry

Miners have transaction ordering power, but traders do not. Anti-MEV solutions must address this imbalance.

Decentralized Execution

The process of executing transactions should be decentralized and not tied to any single entity to prevent concentrated power.

Verifiable Outcomes

All outcomes must be publicly verifiable to ensure that the rules of the protocol are being followed by all participants.

2. Proposer-Builder Separation (PBS)

Making the Thief the Judge

A Redistribution of Profits

PBS does not fundamentally constrain the miner's transaction ordering power; it is merely a redistribution of MEV profits.

Who Are the Real Attackers?

  • Ordinary retail users do not have transaction ordering power, and if a transaction fails, they still have to pay gas fees.
  • Ethereum miners are the biggest beneficiaries of MEV, as they control the ordering power and almost never fail at MEV.

PBS Exacerbates Asymmetry

  • Some private relays do not publicize their mempools, which itself blocks a trader's right to know.
  • Resource-rich builders will gradually squeeze other builders out of the market. https://explorer.rated.network/builders?network=mainnet&timeWindow=1d&page=1

PBS Builder Concentration

The Winner-Takes-All Scenario

The essence of PBS is to eliminate small-scale MEV attackers (retail users) and aggregate large-scale MEV attackers (the miner collective). Superficially, the number of successful MEV attacks may seem to decrease, but in reality, it's a winner-takes-all scenario.

3. FOCIL: Burying Your Head in the Sand

Committee Conflicts of Interest

In FOCIL, the committee is also selected from Ethereum beacon validators, which is like choosing a referee from the players in a game.

A New Form of MEV Attack

The method of forcibly stuffing the committee's "IL" (Insertion List) transactions into a block is, in itself, a form of MEV attack.

4. Encrypted Mempools Are Not Mature

Common Problems with Encryption

  • The decryption performance of asymmetric encryption is extremely poor; RSA, for example, is on the order of 100ms.
  • High-performance symmetric encryption algorithms will cause a massive increase in storage costs for both individuals and public chains.

Problems with Time-Locked Encryption

  • The difficulty of setting the time lock is problematic. If the time is < average network latency, the effect is minimal; if it is > public network latency, it will affect block production and throughput.
  • In the pursuit of faster decryption, miners will continuously upgrade their chips, which will strengthen the trend towards centralization.

Threshold Encryption & TEE

  • Threshold Encryption Problems: Trust Assumption (Committee)
  • TEE (Trusted Execution Environment) Problems: Trust Assumption (Hardware)

5. Anti-MEV Solutions Should Not Be Concentrated

Solely on Ethereum L1

Engineering Requires Layering

  • An operating system doesn't need to care about a database's atomicity and consistency problems; it only needs to ensure its own file system is reliable.
  • A banking system should only be used to define, issue, and settle currency. It should not take on the business of a stock exchange and then try to solve the technical challenges of a stock exchange within its own system.

How Blockchains Need to Be Layered

L1 should only be responsible for settlement and proof of the legality of state changes. Other business logic should be handled by other application-specific chains, as different businesses have different infrastructure requirements. Some need anti-MEV measures, some do not; some need high throughput, while others prioritize low latency; some need to establish their own trusted data sources, and some need to design a separate consensus mechanism.

6. My Solution

A Layered Approach for DeFi

Only perform settlement on L1. DeFi requires a separate application-specific chain: Odd-numbered blocks are used for transaction ordering, and even-numbered blocks are used for transaction execution.

The Solution Workflow

  • The user first sends the transaction hash (txHash) to the chain and prepays a small fee.
  • Miners order these txHashes based on the prepaid fees and broadcast their ordering (making an ordering commitment).
  • After seeing the ordering commitment, the user sends the full transaction content.
  • The miners complete the transaction execution based on the previous ordering commitment.

Anti-MEV Solution Architecture

Fulfilling the Three Elements

  • Weakening the Power Asymmetry: Miners do not know the transaction content before ordering, so they cannot perform MEV.
  • Decentralized Execution: Using txHash instead of encrypted transactions saves more bandwidth and computing resources.
  • Verifiable Outcomes: Ordering is based on the prepaid fee amount, and the ordering commitment is confirmed before execution.
